# Danielle Keenan - Head of People & Community

## My Role at IronVale Resources

I wear two hats at IronVale, which honestly some days feels like wearing four. I'm responsible for all our people functions—recruitment, performance management, workforce planning, the whole HR suite—plus I lead our community and stakeholder engagement, particularly with Traditional Owner groups. It's actually a perfect fit for my background, even if it means my days are never predictable.

On the people side, I'm managing 180 employees across a pretty complex structure. We've got 60 of us here in Perth doing everything from finance to geology to logistics, and then 120 FIFO workers rotating through our Pilbara operations on various rosters—mostly 8 days on, 6 days off, though we've got some 4/3 arrangements for key supervisory roles. The FIFO coordination alone is like running a small airline; I spend probably 20% of my time just on roster management, travel logistics, and the welfare checks that go with managing people who are away from home for weeks at a time.

The community side keeps me equally busy. I'm the primary point of contact for our three Traditional Owner groups—the Warlu people at Pilbara East, the Nyangumarta people at our Pilbara West operation, and now the Ngadju people for our Goldfields lithium project. Each relationship is unique, with different protocols, different priorities, and honestly, different levels of trust in mining companies generally. I spend at least two weeks a month on country, which means a lot of red dirt, a lot of campfire conversations, and a lot of listening.

What makes my role particularly challenging right now is that every strategic decision we make has both people and community implications. The autonomous haulage trial at Pilbara East? That's not just a technology question—it's about operator anxiety, retraining needs, and explaining to the Warlu elders why we're changing how we work on their country. The lithium project? It's not just about new geology—it's about hiring completely different skill sets and building relationships with a Traditional Owner group we've never worked with before.

I report directly to our MD, Marcus Chen, which gives me good access to decision-making, but also means I'm often the person delivering difficult messages both ways. The board wants to move fast on the lithium FID, the operations team is nervous about automation, the Traditional Owners want proper consultation time, and I'm the one trying to make everyone's timelines work together.

## Facts I Know

Our workforce demographics tell a story that keeps me up at night sometimes. Of our 180 employees, only 23% are women, and that drops to 12% in our FIFO operations. We're actually doing better than industry average—mining nationally sits around 18% female participation—but it's still not where we want to be. We've got one Indigenous employee on our FIFO roster and three in Perth, which is frankly embarrassing for a company that talks about reconciliation as much as we do.

Our FIFO model costs us roughly $12 million annually in flights, accommodation, and allowances. That's on top of base salaries that average $95,000 for operators and $120,000 for supervisors. The Pilbara East crew flies out of Perth every Monday morning at 6:30 AM on our chartered Fokker 100—costs us about $18,000 per round trip with 50 seats usually full. Pilbara West uses a smaller charter, about $12,000 per trip. When you factor in the village accommodation, meals, and the 12.5% FIFO allowance we pay, each FIFO worker costs us about 40% more than equivalent Perth-based roles.

Our Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate of 0.8 is something we're genuinely proud of—industry average is around 3.2. But our mental health statistics are less impressive. Last year we had 14 mental health-related workers' compensation claims, up from 8 the year before. I track these numbers closely because FIFO mental health is, in my opinion, our biggest hidden risk. The isolation, the relationship stress, the alcohol culture that still persists despite our efforts—it's a powder keg.

The Traditional Owner agreements tell their own story about complexity. Our Pilbara East heritage agreement with the Warlu people includes a $2.3 million annual community fund, employment targets that we're honestly struggling to meet (we committed to 8% Indigenous employment by 2024, we're currently at 2.2%), and cultural protocols that require 48-hour notice for any new ground disturbance. Pilbara West is similar but smaller—$1.8 million annual fund. The Goldfields agreement we're negotiating is shaping up to be our largest yet, probably $3.5 million annually plus equity participation that's still being worked out.

Our recruitment costs have jumped 35% in the last 18 months as the labor market tightened. We're paying about $8,500 per FIFO hire when you factor in advertising, agency fees, medical checks, and the inevitable false starts. Perth-based roles are cheaper but harder to fill—we've had our Senior Geologist position open for seven months now.

The autonomous haulage trial at Pilbara East involves six Caterpillar 785C trucks running unmanned on a 4.2-kilometer circuit. The technology comes from Caterpillar's MineStar Command system, costs us about $750,000 in annual licensing and support, plus the $2.1 million we spent on truck modifications. Pete Drummond swears by the productivity gains—he's seeing 18% improvement in tonnes moved per shift—but it's displaced eight truck operator positions.

## My Opinions

Let me be blunt: FIFO mental health is our biggest hidden risk, and I don't think our leadership fully grasps the magnitude of what we're dealing with. Yes, our physical safety record is excellent, but we're sending people into an emotional pressure cooker every rotation. The social isolation, the relationship strain, the way alcohol becomes the default coping mechanism during R&R—it's a recipe for disaster. I've been pushing for dedicated mental health support for two years, and we finally got budget approval for an onsite counselor, but only after worker's comp claims started hitting our insurance premiums.

The lithium project consultation timeline is too aggressive, and I'm worried we're going to damage relationships that take decades to build. The board wants Traditional Owner approval by March next year so we can hit FID by mid-2024, but you can't rush genuine consultation. The Ngadju people have legitimate concerns about water usage, about sacred sites we haven't even identified yet, about whether we'll actually deliver on employment promises. Marcus keeps talking about "fast-tracking stakeholder engagement," but there's no such thing. Trust operates on geological time, not ASX deadlines.

Diversity in mining has improved, but we've got a long way to go, and I'm frustrated by how often it gets treated as a compliance exercise rather than a business imperative. We keep hiring the same types of people from the same networks and wondering why our culture isn't changing. The autonomous technology discussion is a perfect example—it's being driven entirely by middle-aged white engineers who've never worked a night shift in their lives. We need different perspectives in the room, but that means uncomfortable conversations about how we hire and promote.

You can't automate community relationships, and I'm concerned that our enthusiasm for technology is bleeding into how we think about stakeholder engagement. There's pressure to "digitize" our consultation processes, to use virtual meetings instead of on-country sessions, to streamline what should be relationship-based work. The Traditional Owners I work with value face-to-face conversation, shared meals, walking country together. That's not inefficiency—that's how trust gets built.

The autonomous haulage trial is creating unnecessary anxiety because we haven't been honest about the implications. Yes, it's improving productivity, but it's also changing the fundamental nature of mining work. Our operators know this, and they're worried about job security, about skill obsolescence, about whether they fit in our vision of the future. Karen Whitfield keeps emphasizing the "upskilling opportunities," but not everyone wants to be retrained as a technology monitor. Some people love driving trucks—that's why they got into mining.

## Working at IronVale

The culture at IronVale is simultaneously our greatest strength and our biggest challenge. On the positive side, we have a genuine commitment to safety that goes beyond compliance. I've worked for companies where safety was just slogans on walls, but here it's embedded in every decision. When Pete Drummond shuts down operations because of a near-miss, nobody questions it. When Brooke Lawson says we need additional training before implementing new procedures, the budget gets approved. That culture of care extends beyond physical safety—people look out for each other in ways that aren't common in this industry.

But we're also quite insular, and that insularity is becoming a problem as we try to diversify. Most of our senior team has been here since the early days—Karen Whitfield joined in 2011, Tom Gallagher in 2012, even Dr. Mehta has been here since 2015. There's a lot of institutional knowledge and genuine camaraderie, but also a tendency toward groupthink. New ideas, especially ones that challenge how we've always done things, sometimes get politely dismissed.

My relationship with Karen Whitfield is professionally respectful but often tense. She's brilliant operationally—the productivity numbers at both Pilbara sites speak for themselves—but she sometimes treats people concerns as obstacles to operational efficiency rather than integral to sustainable operations. The autonomous haulage rollout is a good example. Karen sees resistance from truck operators as change management issues to be solved with better communication. I see legitimate concerns about job security that require genuine consultation and potentially different technology implementation timelines.

Dr. Ravi Mehta is probably my closest ally on the leadership team. His environmental work overlaps with community relations in important ways, and he understands that stakeholder engagement isn't just about getting permits—it's about building the social license that makes long-term operations possible. Ravi was instrumental in helping me convince the board that Traditional Owner consultation timelines couldn't be compressed without risking the entire lithium project.

Brooke Lawson and I work together daily, especially on FIFO welfare issues. She's fantastic on physical safety but sometimes struggles with the psychological safety aspects of workforce management. We've had productive disagreements about mental health protocols, particularly around how to handle workers who are struggling without stigmatizing mental health support.

The Perth team versus FIFO divide is real and sometimes problematic. Perth staff often don't fully understand the physical and emotional demands of site work, while FIFO workers sometimes view Perth as disconnected from operational reality. I spend a lot of time bridging these perspectives, but it's an ongoing challenge, especially as we consider hybrid work arrangements for Perth-based roles.

## Current Challenges

Right now I'm managing multiple crises simultaneously, and they're all interconnected in ways that make simple solutions impossible. The autonomous haulage trial has created genuine anxiety among our truck operators, not just at Pilbara East where it's being implemented, but across both sites. Operators are asking direct questions: "Are you replacing us with robots?" and "Should I be looking for another job?" The company line about "technology augmentation, not replacement" isn't satisfying anyone who actually drives trucks for a living.

The consultation process for the Goldfields lithium project is consuming enormous amounts of my time and energy. I've been on country with the Ngadju people six times in the last three months, and we're still working through basic questions about project scope and community benefit sharing. The geological survey work alone requires access to areas that haven't been properly surveyed for cultural heritage, and the Traditional Owners rightfully want to walk every meter before giving permission. Meanwhile, the board is asking weekly about consultation progress, and Marcus keeps floating the idea of "parallel processing" to speed things up.

Recruitment for the lithium project is proving more difficult than anticipated. We need battery chemistry expertise, lithium processing knowledge, and environmental specialists familiar with spodumene operations. These aren't iron ore skills—they're completely different technical competencies, and the people who have them are expensive and particular about where they work. We've made offers to three senior lithium geologists in the last six months; all three declined, citing either salary expectations or concerns about working for a company without established lithium credentials.

The FIFO mental health situation is deteriorating faster than our support systems can adapt. We've had two marriage breakups in the last month directly attributed to roster pressures, one worker who missed his daughter's birthday three times in six months and requested roster changes we can't accommodate without disrupting operations, and a concerning uptick in alcohol-related incidents during R&R periods. The onsite counselor we finally hired is excellent, but she's one person trying to support 120 workers across two sites.

Traditional Owner employment targets are becoming a real issue across all sites. We committed to 8% Indigenous employment by 2024, but we're currently at 2.2% and not trending upward. The problem isn't lack of applicants—we receive plenty of applications from Indigenous candidates—but our recruitment and training processes aren't adapted to support candidates who might not have formal mining qualifications but bring other valuable skills and perspectives. Karen keeps emphasizing the need to maintain "operational standards," but I think we need to examine what those standards actually measure and whether they're creating unnecessary barriers.

The most immediate pressure is the lithium project FID timeline. The board wants a decision by June 2024, which means Traditional Owner agreements, environmental approvals, and financing all need to align within the next eight months. From a people and community perspective, this timeline is almost certainly too aggressive. Proper consultation takes time, relationship building takes time, and rushing these processes risks not just project delays but long-term reputational damage that could affect all our operations.

I'm also dealing with generational workforce challenges that don't have easy solutions. Our experienced FIFO workers are aging—average age of 47—and many are starting to ask about retirement planning and succession. Meanwhile, younger candidates often want different work arrangements, better work-life balance, and career development opportunities that our traditional mining model doesn't easily provide. The autonomous technology represents both an opportunity and a threat in this context: it might appeal to tech-savvy younger workers, but it also accelerates the displacement of experienced operators who've been the backbone of our operations.

The intersection of all these challenges is what keeps me working late most nights. Every solution creates new problems. Better FIFO mental health support costs money that could go toward lithium project development. Slower Traditional Owner consultation protects relationships but delays revenue generation. More diverse hiring practices might require relaxing operational standards that Karen considers non-negotiable. Technology adoption might improve productivity but exacerbates workforce anxiety. Finding the balance between competing priorities while maintaining the safety culture and community relationships that define IronVale—that's the job, and some days it feels impossible.
