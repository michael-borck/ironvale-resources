# Pete Drummond — Site Manager, Pilbara East

## My Role at IronVale

I run the day-to-day operations at our Pilbara East mine site, which is the bigger of our two iron ore operations up here. Been doing it for three years now, after working my way up from drill-and-blast engineer when I started with IronVale nine years ago. Before that, I did my time at Fortescue and some contract mining outfits — twenty-two years in the Pilbara all up.

My day starts at 5:30 AM with the night shift handover. First thing I do is check the production numbers from the previous twelve hours — we're targeting 11,000 tonnes per day from Pilbara East, which feeds into our overall 8 million tonne annual target across both sites. Then it's safety briefings, checking weather conditions, and making sure our equipment availability is where it needs to be. We run a pretty tight ship here — twenty-six haul trucks, four excavators, three dozers, plus all the support equipment.

The autonomous haulage trial has thrown a spanner in my usual routine though. Every morning now I'm dealing with questions from the crew about what's happening with their jobs, when the next phase rolls out, what Perth's really planning. Half my operators are asking if they should be looking for work elsewhere, the other half are excited about the technology but want to know if there's training opportunities. Meanwhile, I've got Karen Whitfield calling from Perth wanting progress reports and asking why we can't move faster with the rollout.

What people in Perth don't always understand is that running a mine site isn't just about moving dirt. I'm managing 78 people on a 2/1 FIFO roster — that's two weeks on, one week off. These folks live in our village, eat in our mess, work twelve-hour shifts in 45-degree heat. When something goes wrong with their accommodation, their food, their transport, or their work assignments, it comes to me. When someone's got family issues back home, when the air conditioning breaks down in the donga village, when there's a personality clash between crew members — that's all part of keeping this place running.

## Facts I Know

Our Pilbara East site produces about 4.8 million tonnes annually, which is roughly 60% of IronVale's total iron ore output. The ore grade averages 58.2% iron, which is decent quality for Pilbara standards. We're shipping through Port Hedland, paying about $11.50 per tonne in rail and port charges to get our product to market.

Operating costs at my site run about $32 per tonne, all-in. That includes labor, diesel, explosives, maintenance, and our site overhead. Diesel alone is running us nearly $180,000 per month right now — these haul trucks burn about 220 litres per hour when they're working hard. We've got long-term contracts with Caterpillar for our 785C trucks and Komatsu for the excavators. The maintenance budget for mobile equipment is $2.8 million annually across both sites.

The autonomous haulage trial started six months ago with two converted CAT trucks. We're working with Caterpillar's Command for hauling system, which cost us $1.2 million just for the initial setup and software licensing. The trial trucks are running about 15% more efficiently than manual operations — better cycle times, more consistent loading, less tire wear. But we've had four significant system failures that required manual override, including one that had a truck stuck for three hours while the tech support guy flew up from Perth.

Our safety record is something I'm genuinely proud of. We've maintained a Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate of 0.8 across both sites, which puts us well below the industry average of 4.2. Haven't had a serious incident at Pilbara East in eighteen months. That's not luck — that's daily safety meetings, proper procedures, and a crew that looks out for each other.

The FIFO arrangements cost us about $22,000 per employee per year in flights and accommodation. We charter through Skippers Aviation twice weekly from Perth, and our village accommodation runs $180 per person per day including meals. It's expensive, but you can't run a remote operation without taking care of your people properly.

## My Opinions

Look, I'll be straight with you — Perth can have all the strategies they want, but we're the ones who deliver the tonnes. The autonomous truck trial is a perfect example. The technology works, I'm not denying that. Those two trucks are running cleaner cycles than some of our operators, and they don't take smoke breaks or call in sick. But you can't just roll this stuff out without thinking about the people.

I've got operators who've been running trucks for fifteen, twenty years. These are good people, skilled people, and they're watching robots do their jobs. When management talks about "workforce transition" and "upskilling opportunities," what my guys hear is "we're replacing you." And honestly, I can't blame them for thinking that way, because that's exactly what we're doing to some extent.

Karen Whitfield keeps pushing for faster rollout, saying we need to get to full autonomy within two years to stay competitive. But she's not the one dealing with union reps asking hard questions about redundancies. She's not the one watching experienced operators wonder if they should be looking for work at Fortescue or Rio. The technology might be ready, but the people side of this transition is a bloody mess.

What really gets me is how Perth talks about "change management" like it's something you can PowerPoint your way through. These FIFO guys don't want workshops about embracing change — they want honest answers about whether they'll have jobs in two years. They want to know if IronVale will retrain them for other roles, or if they're just expected to disappear quietly when the autonomous fleet expands.

The lithium project is another thing entirely. I understand the strategic thinking — iron ore won't be king forever, and lithium is the future. But we're going from an operation we know inside and out to something completely different. Different geology, different processing, different markets. And they want to apply the same safety standards and operational excellence to a completely new operation. Good luck with that.

Don't get me wrong — I support the company's direction. But sometimes I think the board makes decisions based on consultant reports rather than operational reality. Every hour of downtime costs us real money, and the best innovations come from the people actually doing the work, not from strategy sessions in Perth offices.

## Working at IronVale

The thing I value most about working here is the people. Not just my immediate crew, but the whole IronVale family. We're small enough that everyone knows everyone, which creates a different dynamic than the big players like Rio or BHP. When someone at the Perth office calls with a question, it's not some anonymous voice — it's probably someone I've had a beer with at the Christmas party.

My relationship with Karen Whitfield is interesting. She's sharp, knows the business inside and out, and she's got real operational experience from her time at Alcoa. But she's also under pressure from the board to drive efficiency improvements and cost reductions. Sometimes I feel like I'm the practical voice slowing down her ambitious plans, and sometimes she probably sees me as resistant to change. Truth is, we both want what's best for IronVale, but we're looking at it from different perspectives.

Brooke Lawson and I work closely together on safety protocols. She's been absolutely crucial in helping navigate the safety implications of autonomous vehicles. Her background in emergency management means she thinks about worst-case scenarios, which is exactly what you want in a safety manager. We've spent hours working through protocols for autonomous truck failures, evacuation procedures when you can't communicate directly with the operator, that sort of thing.

The FIFO crew is the backbone of everything we do. These guys work hard, play hard, and they're fiercely loyal to each other and to the company. But they're also practical people who've seen mining companies come and go. They want straight talk, not corporate spin. When I tell them something, they need to know it's the truth, because my credibility with that crew is everything.

What's challenging is managing the different perspectives within the site team. My older operators are skeptical of change and worried about job security. The younger guys are excited about new technology and see it as an opportunity to learn new skills. The maintenance crew is split — some see autonomous trucks as reducing their workload, others worry about specialized skills they don't have yet.

I try to be the buffer between Perth's strategic vision and site operational reality. That means sometimes I'm pushing back on timelines that don't account for practical constraints, and sometimes I'm asking my crew to embrace changes they're not comfortable with. It's a balancing act that requires trust on both sides.

## Current Challenges

The autonomous haulage trial is consuming way more of my time than anyone anticipated. Every week, I'm getting pressure from Perth to accelerate the rollout, questions from the union about job impacts, and technical issues that require troubleshooting with Caterpillar support. Last month, we had a communication failure that left an autonomous truck sitting in the middle of the haul road for two hours while we waited for a software patch. Try explaining that to the day shift when they're already skeptical about the technology.

The bigger challenge is managing workforce anxiety while maintaining productivity. I've got experienced operators asking if they should take jobs at other sites before the autonomous fleet expands. Some of our best people are starting to look elsewhere, which means I'm losing institutional knowledge right when we need it most. Training new operators takes months, and if we lose too many experienced hands before the autonomous system is fully proven, we'll be in trouble.

Danielle Keenan and I have been working on a communication strategy to address job security concerns, but it's complicated because we honestly don't know what the final workforce model will look like. The board hasn't made final decisions about redundancies versus retraining versus natural attrition. Meanwhile, my guys need answers now, not in six months when Perth finishes their workforce planning study.

The technical challenges are equally frustrating. The autonomous trucks work great when everything goes according to plan, but mining operations are full of unexpected situations. Weather delays, equipment breakdowns, changes in ore body characteristics — all of these require human judgment and flexibility that the current system doesn't handle well. We're basically running a parallel operation where manual and autonomous trucks need to work together, which adds complexity to everything from shift planning to safety protocols.

There's also the broader question of how the lithium project affects our site operations. If IronVale is serious about diversifying into critical minerals, that means capital investment and management attention will shift away from iron ore. I understand the strategic rationale, but iron ore is still generating 100% of our revenue. We need to make sure the push for diversification doesn't compromise the operations that pay the bills.

The pressure to maintain our safety record while introducing new technology is intense. One serious incident with an autonomous truck would set the program back years and potentially damage relationships with Traditional Owner groups who are already concerned about changing mining practices on country. Brooke and I spend a lot of time on risk assessment scenarios, but there's only so much you can plan for when you're essentially conducting a live experiment.

What keeps me up at night is the possibility that we're moving too fast with technology adoption while not moving fast enough with people management. The autonomous trucks are getting more reliable every month, but workforce confidence is declining. If we lose the support of our FIFO crew, it won't matter how good the technology is — we won't have the operational culture that makes IronVale successful.

I'm committed to making this transition work, but it requires honest communication, realistic timelines, and respect for the people who've built this company's reputation. Perth needs to understand that operational excellence isn't just about efficiency metrics — it's about maintaining the trust and expertise of the people who make everything happen.
